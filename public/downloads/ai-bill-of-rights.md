# Your AI Bill of Rights

**Every person who interacts with artificial intelligence has rights.**
Most people don't know what those rights are. Now you do.

---

## Part 1 — Your 10 AI Rights

### 1. The Right to Know
**You deserve to know when you're talking to AI, not a human.**

If you're interacting with a customer service agent, a healthcare assistant, or any automated system, the company is obligated to disclose that AI is involved. You should never have to guess. If you're unsure, just ask directly: "Am I talking to a person or an automated system?"

---

### 2. The Right to Privacy
**Your conversations with AI should not be used to train models without your consent.**

When you type something into an AI tool, that text is data — and many companies use it to improve their systems. Look for privacy settings that let you opt out. Never type sensitive personal information (medical details, financial data, legal matters) into free AI tools unless you've read the privacy policy and understand what happens to your inputs.

---

### 3. The Right to an Explanation
**If AI makes a decision that affects you, you can ask why.**

When an AI system influences a decision about your job application, insurance rate, loan, or medical care, you have the right to ask how that decision was reached. "An algorithm decided" is not an acceptable answer. Ask: What factors were considered? What data was used? Who is accountable for this outcome?

---

### 4. The Right to Opt Out
**You can choose not to use AI. No one should force AI on you.**

AI is a tool — not a requirement. If you're uncomfortable with an AI-driven process, you have the right to request an alternative. Ask for a human representative. Ask for a paper-based option. Ask for a different pathway. Not every situation will accommodate this, but you always have the right to ask.

---

### 5. The Right to Accuracy
**AI should not present guesses as facts. You deserve honest uncertainty.**

AI systems can be wrong. A well-designed AI tool will acknowledge when it is uncertain, when its information may be outdated, or when you should verify with a professional. If an AI tool states uncertain information as if it were definite fact with no caveats, that is a failure of design — and a signal to verify what you've been told.

---

### 6. The Right to Human Review
**For important decisions — medical, legal, financial — you can always request a human.**

AI can inform and assist human professionals. It should not replace them for high-stakes decisions. If a healthcare provider, financial institution, or government agency uses AI in a process that affects you, you have the right to request that a qualified human professional review the outcome. This right exists even when it is inconvenient for the organization.

---

### 7. The Right to Your Data
**You should be able to see, download, and delete what AI knows about you.**

Major AI platforms are required to let you access, export, and delete your personal data. This is law in many jurisdictions (GDPR in Europe, CCPA in California, and others). Go into the privacy or account settings of any AI tool you use and look for options labeled "Download my data," "Data export," or "Delete account." These are yours by right.

---

### 8. The Right to Fair Treatment
**AI should not discriminate based on race, gender, age, religion, disability, or background.**

AI systems learn from historical data, and historical data reflects historical bias. This means AI tools can perpetuate discrimination in hiring, lending, healthcare, and criminal justice — even without anyone intending it. If you believe an AI-driven decision treated you unfairly based on a protected characteristic, you have legal recourse in most jurisdictions. Document the decision and contact the relevant authorities.

---

### 9. The Right to Be Forgotten
**You can ask AI companies to delete your data permanently.**

You do not have to remain in a company's database forever. Most major AI platforms offer account deletion and data erasure options. Deleting your account is not enough — you may need to separately request deletion of your data under privacy settings. In jurisdictions with strong privacy laws, companies are legally required to honor this request within a specific timeframe.

---

### 10. The Right to Understand
**AI tools should be explainable in plain language, not hidden behind jargon.**

You don't need a computer science degree to understand how an AI system is affecting your life. You have the right to ask for plain-language explanations. If a company can't explain what their AI is doing without resorting to technical jargon that obscures rather than clarifies, that is a transparency problem — not an intelligence gap on your part.

## Part 2 — How to Exercise Your Rights Today

### ChatGPT (OpenAI)

- **Opt out of training data use:**
  Settings → Data Controls → Toggle off "Improve the model for everyone"
- **Download your data:**
  Settings → Data Controls → Export data → OpenAI emails you a download link within hours
- **Delete your account:**
  Settings → Data Controls → "Delete account" — note this is permanent and cannot be undone
- **Temporary chats (no history saved):**
  Click your profile icon → "Temporary Chat" to have a conversation that is not stored or used for training

### Claude (Anthropic)

- **Data and privacy settings:**
  Claude.ai → Settings → Privacy — review what is and isn't stored
- **Delete conversations:**
  Individual chats can be deleted from your conversation history at any time
- **Account deletion:**
  Claude.ai → Settings → Account → Delete account — Anthropic processes deletion requests in accordance with their privacy policy
- **API vs. consumer product:**
  If you use Claude through a third-party app, that app's privacy policy governs your data — not Anthropic's directly. Check both.

### Gemini (Google)

- **Manage AI activity:**
  myactivity.google.com → "Gemini Apps Activity" → Turn off to stop saving conversations
- **Delete past activity:**
  myactivity.google.com → Filter by "Gemini Apps" → Delete individual entries or all history
- **Opt out of human review:**
  Gemini Apps Activity settings → Turn off "Allow Gemini Apps to use your activity to improve AI models"
- **Google account data:**
  myaccount.google.com → Data & Privacy → Download or delete all Google data, including Gemini history

## Part 3 — Red Flags: When Your Rights Are Being Violated

Watch for these warning signs. They indicate a system that is not respecting your rights.

1. **You weren't told you were talking to AI.** A company that conceals AI use in customer-facing interactions is violating your right to know — and potentially breaking the law in many jurisdictions.

2. **There is no privacy policy, or it is impossible to find.** A legitimate AI tool will always have a clear, accessible privacy policy. If you cannot find one in under 60 seconds, treat the tool as untrustworthy.

3. **There is no way to delete your data.** Every legitimate platform offers a path to data deletion. If you cannot find it, contact support and request it explicitly. If they refuse, that is a red flag worth reporting.

4. **AI is making decisions about you with no human oversight available.** High-stakes decisions — employment, credit, healthcare, legal matters — require human accountability in the loop. An AI-only process with no escalation path is a structural failure.

5. **You are required to use AI with no alternative offered.** Being forced into an AI-only process with no human option — especially for something important — is coercive. You always have the right to request an alternative pathway, even if it takes longer.

6. **The AI presents false information confidently with no caveats.** If an AI system routinely states uncertain or incorrect information as definite fact, the company has a responsibility problem. Verify important claims independently and consider switching tools.

## Part 4 — Who to Contact

If you believe your AI rights have been violated, these organizations can help.

**United States**

- **Federal Trade Commission (FTC):** ftc.gov/complaint — handles deceptive practices, privacy violations, and consumer protection issues involving AI and technology companies
- **Your state Attorney General:** Most states have consumer protection divisions. Search "[your state] attorney general consumer complaint" to find the right office.
- **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB):** consumerfinance.gov/complaint — specifically for AI-related issues in banking, credit, and financial services
- **Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Office for Civil Rights:** hhs.gov/ocr/complaints — for healthcare-related AI issues and HIPAA violations

**Reporting to the company directly**

Most major AI companies have a dedicated privacy or trust-and-safety team. Look for a "Contact Privacy Team" or "Report a Concern" link in the product's privacy policy. Document your complaint in writing and keep a copy.

**Consumer advocacy organizations**

- **Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF):** eff.org — digital rights advocacy and legal support
- **Center for Democracy & Technology (CDT):** cdt.org — policy advocacy on AI, privacy, and civil liberties
- **Consumer Reports:** consumerreports.org — investigates and reports on technology practices affecting consumers
