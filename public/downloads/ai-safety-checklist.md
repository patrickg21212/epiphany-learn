# AI Safety Checklist
### Your Practical Guide to Using AI Confidently

*A free resource from Epiphany Dynamics — epiphanydynamics.ai*

## 🔴 Section 1: Never Share With AI

**These items should never be typed into any AI chatbot — free or paid.**

- [ ] **Social Security Number (SSN)** — no legitimate AI task requires it
- [ ] **Passwords or PINs** — for any account, device, or system
- [ ] **Bank account or routing numbers**
- [ ] **Credit or debit card numbers** (including CVV codes)
- [ ] **Medical records or diagnosis details** — symptoms are usually fine; full records are not
- [ ] **Private photos or images of other people** — especially minors
- [ ] **Confidential work documents** — contracts, internal memos, client data, trade secrets
- [ ] **Passport or driver's license numbers**
- [ ] **Immigration status information**
- [ ] **Login credentials for any account** (email, banking, social media)

> **Why:** Even well-designed AI systems store conversation data for training and review. There is no guarantee your input stays private.

## 🟡 Section 2: Think Twice Before Sharing

**These are okay in some situations, but carry real risk depending on context and platform.**

- [ ] **Your full name + city or address together** — either alone is usually fine; combined creates a profile
- [ ] **Your employer's name + specific internal details** — mentioning your industry is low risk; specific project names or client names may violate NDAs
- [ ] **Your children's names, ages, or school details** — vague references are fine; specifics build a profile of a minor
- [ ] **Ongoing legal situations** — AI can explain general law, but sharing case specifics is risky
- [ ] **Details about other people without their knowledge** — asking AI to help draft a message about a coworker or neighbor shares their information without consent
- [ ] **Health conditions of family members** — asking general questions is fine; detailed family medical history is not
- [ ] **Financial situation specifics** — general budgeting questions are fine; sharing exact income, debt amounts, or account balances is not

> **Rule of thumb:** Ask yourself — "If this conversation were printed and left on a coffee shop table, would I be uncomfortable?" If yes, leave it out.

---

## 🟢 Section 3: Generally Safe to Share

**These types of information carry low risk and are what AI tools are designed to help with.**

- [ ] **General how-to questions** — "How do I write a cover letter?" or "What's the best way to cook chicken?"
- [ ] **Creative writing prompts and fiction** — characters, plots, made-up worlds
- [ ] **Publicly available information** — things you'd find on Wikipedia or in a news article
- [ ] **Hypothetical scenarios** — "What would happen if a city banned cars?" or "Write a story about a robot"
- [ ] **Learning and research topics** — history, science, math, languages
- [ ] **Your general profession or industry** — "I work in healthcare" or "I'm a small business owner" without sensitive specifics
- [ ] **Editing and proofreading** — drafts of emails, resumes, or letters that contain no sensitive data
- [ ] **Ideas and brainstorming** — product names, event planning, gift ideas

## 🔍 Section 4: How to Spot AI-Generated Fakes

### Fake Images — Look For:
- **Hands with the wrong number of fingers** (AI frequently gets this wrong — count them)
- **Text in the image that is blurry, misspelled, or nonsensical** when you zoom in
- **Backgrounds that don't make sense** — architecture that curves oddly, trees that merge together
- **Ears, hair, and jewelry** that are asymmetrical or seem to dissolve into the background
- **Eyes that are slightly "glassy"** or reflect light from two different directions
- **Watermarks from image generators** (look for faint logos in corners or edges)

### Fake Text and Articles — Look For:
- **No named author, no publication date, or no source citations**
- **Writing that is unusually smooth and never uses contractions or casual phrases** — human writing has rhythm and imperfections
- **Claims that sound plausible but have no linked evidence**
- **Overly perfect grammar** with zero personality
- **Statistics or quotes with no verifiable origin** — if you can't find who said it or where the number came from, be skeptical

### Deepfake Video and Audio — Look For:
- **Lip movements that don't quite match the words** — especially on longer syllables
- **Unnatural blinking** — either too frequent or not frequent enough
- **Lighting that doesn't match** between the person's face and the background
- **Blurry or warped edges** around the face, ears, or hairline
- **Voice that sounds slightly flat** — real voices have breath, hesitation, and natural variation

### AI Voice Clone Scam Calls — Watch For:
- A caller who **claims to be a family member or coworker in distress** and asks for money or personal information
- **Urgency and pressure** — "Don't tell anyone, just send money now"
- **A voice that sounds right but feels slightly off** — small pauses or robotic transitions
- **Establish a family code word** for emergencies that only real family members would know

## ✅ Section 5: Your 30-Second Verification Routine

When you see something suspicious — an alarming photo, a shocking headline, a viral video — run through this before sharing:

**Step 1: Check the source**
Who published this? Is it a site you recognize? Look for an About page, a real author name, and a publication date. If any of those are missing, pause.

**Step 2: Reverse image search**
Right-click the image (on desktop) and select "Search image" or go to images.google.com and drag the photo in. If the same image appears on unrelated stories from years ago, it's been recycled or fabricated.

**Step 3: Look for the original**
Search for the story or claim on a news aggregator like Google News. If multiple credible outlets are covering it, it's more likely real. If only one site has it, or the only results are copies of the same post, be skeptical.

**Step 4: When in doubt, don't share**
You are not obligated to pass along information you can't verify. Sharing a fake does real harm — to real people. Sitting on a story for 24 hours costs you nothing. Spreading a fabricated photo or quote can spread to thousands.

## 💳 Section 6: Free vs. Paid — What's Different About Privacy

| | **Free Tier** | **Paid Tier** |
|---|---|---|
| **ChatGPT (OpenAI)** | Conversations may be used to train future models by default. You can opt out in Settings → Data Controls. | Paid plans offer the same opt-out, and enterprise plans include stronger data protections. Training use is off by default for paid API customers. |
| **Claude (Anthropic)** | Free conversations may be reviewed by humans for safety and quality. Not used for training by default on the consumer app. | Similar to free tier for the consumer app. Enterprise and API plans include stricter data handling agreements. |
| **Gemini (Google)** | Free conversations are reviewed by humans and may be used to improve Google's products. Tied to your Google account. | Google One AI Premium subscribers have access to stricter privacy controls. Workspace customers have enterprise-grade protections. |

**The bottom line:** Paid tiers generally give you more control, but always check the current privacy settings in your account — they change. The safest assumption on any free AI tool is that human reviewers may read your conversations. That's not an alarm — it's just a reason to keep sensitive information out.
